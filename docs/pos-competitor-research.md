# KitchenOps POS Competitor Research

## Summary

KitchenOps should not try to replace POS hardware first. Irish café owners already have a till, terminal, and receipt printer. The wedge is a lightweight operations layer: import POS sales, understand best/worst products, track stock and purchases, calculate simple recipe margins, and keep food-safety records in the same place.

## What Competitors Do Well

- POS providers handle card payments, cash drawers, receipt printers, end-of-day close, product catalogues, and basic sales history.
- Larger restaurant platforms provide excellent kitchen display, staff, stock counts, purchase orders, recipe costing, and integrations.
- Inventory specialists handle supplier pricing, recipes, COGS, and purchase workflows better than simple POS systems.

## What Small Café Owners Dislike

- Switching POS hardware is risky and disruptive.
- Pricing can become unclear once hardware, payment rates, inventory modules, and contracts are added.
- Advanced systems often require too much setup for a small café with a short menu.
- Owners still export spreadsheets or manually reconcile POS, stock, supplier invoices, and food-safety records.
- Food-safety logs live separately from sales and stock decisions.

## Opportunity For KitchenOps

KitchenOps should start as the operations layer around the POS, not the POS hardware itself.

- Import sales from current POS CSV files.
- Show best/worst sellers without a full POS migration.
- Track simple product catalogue, stock items, purchases, and recipe costs.
- Keep FridgeProof food-safety checks included as an operations module.
- Add reports that connect sales, stock attention, margins, reminders, and food-safety issues.
- Mark hardware, payment terminal, cash drawer, and receipt-printer integrations as planned, not live.
